### 1. What is scope in penetration testing?

**Student answer:**  
A formal, upfront agreement that defines exactly what security testing is authorized to assess.

**Feedback:** Correct. Scope is more precisely the documented boundary of the assessment, including authorized assets, objectives, people, locations, data, and techniques.

### 2. What are Rules of Engagement?

**Student answer:**  
The directives, policies, and parameters that govern conduct, boundaries, or interactions.

**Feedback:** Correct. In penetration testing, Rules of Engagement define how the assessment will be performed, including allowed and prohibited actions, testing windows, communications, safety limits, evidence handling, and stop conditions.

**Score:** 8/10

### 3. What is the difference between scope and Rules of Engagement?

**Student answer:**  
The scope defines what is included in a project, including boundaries, assets, and objectives, while the Rules of Engagement define how the work is performed, including methodologies, constraints, and safety guidelines.

**Feedback:** Excellent.

**Score:** 10/10

### 4. Why is an IP address alone not always a complete scope?

**Student answer:**  
An IP address alone often fails as a complete scope because it identifies a network connection rather than a specific device or individual.

**Feedback:** Partly correct. An IP address may not show ownership, shared hosting, cloud resources, systems behind the address, data boundaries, permitted techniques, or third-party dependencies.

**Score:** 7/10

### 5. What is an assumption?

**Student answer:**  
A belief, untested hypothesis, or blind guess about a target environment.

**Feedback:** Correct. An assumption is something believed to be true but not yet verified.

**Score:** 9/10

### 6. What is a constraint?

**Student answer:**  
A constraint is a defined rule or limitation placed on the testing scope, environment, or Rules of Engagement.

**Feedback:** Correct. Examples include time, access, safety limits, unavailable systems, or prohibited techniques.

**Score:** 9/10

### 7. What is a stop condition?

**Student answer:**  
A stop condition is a predefined rule in the Rules of Engagement that dictates when a tester must immediately pause, stop, or escalate current actions.

**Feedback:** Excellent.

**Score:** 10/10

### 8. What should a tester do when two scope documents disagree?

**Student answer:**  
The tester must immediately pause because performing unauthorized testing risks legal trouble and accidental system disruption.

**Feedback:** Correct. The tester should pause and obtain written clarification from an authorized representative before testing the disputed area.

**Score:** 9/10

### 9. Name three items that should be included in Rules of Engagement.

**Student answer:**  
Scope, testing windows, and deconfliction or communication protocols.

**Feedback:** Excellent. Other examples include allowed techniques, prohibited actions, emergency contacts, evidence handling, notification rules, and cleanup requirements.

**Score:** 10/10

### 10. Why must third-party systems receive separate authorization?

**Student answer:**  
Third-party systems require separate authorization to prevent unauthorized access and legally protect all parties.

**Feedback:** Excellent. The client may use the service, but that does not automatically give the client or tester permission to assess infrastructure owned by someone else.

**Score:** 10/10

## Corrected Recall Answers

1. Scope is the documented boundary of a penetration test. It identifies the authorized assets, objectives, people, locations, data, and techniques, as well as what is out of scope.

2. Rules of Engagement are the operational rules governing how the test is performed, including allowed and prohibited actions, testing windows, communication, stop conditions, evidence handling, and cleanup.

3. Scope defines **what and where** the tester may assess. Rules of Engagement define **how and when** the assessment may be performed.

4. An IP address alone may not establish ownership, shared infrastructure, cloud or third-party services, data boundaries, systems behind the address, or which techniques are permitted.

5. An assumption is something believed to be true but not yet verified.

6. A constraint is a limit affecting planned work, such as time, access, safety, unavailable systems, or prohibited actions.

7. A stop condition is a predefined event that requires testing to pause immediately and possibly be escalated.

8. When scope documents disagree, pause and obtain written clarification from an authorized representative before continuing.

9. Rules of Engagement should include items such as the testing window, permitted techniques, prohibited actions, emergency contacts, evidence handling, communication procedures, and cleanup requirements.

10. Third-party systems require separate authorization because the client may not own them or have authority to approve security testing against them.
